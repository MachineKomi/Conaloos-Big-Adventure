# Writing Style Guide

Distilled from `GDD.md §5` and `CLAUDE.md §4`. Read this before writing a single line of dialogue.

---

## The voice

A kindly, slightly bewildered traveller who is *also* seeing the world for the first time. Warm, curious, a little cheeky. Never sarcastic. Never mean. Never lecturing.

## The form

- **Predominantly anapestic tetrameter.** *da-da-DUM da-da-DUM da-da-DUM da-da-DUM.*
  Loosen for natural speech, but the underlying pulse should be felt. If a line scans iambically (*da-DUM da-DUM*), it usually means it's two syllables short and a verb away from working.
- **Mostly AABB couplets.** Occasional limerick (AABBA) when a character is being theatrical. ABAB is harder for kids to feel; avoid.
- **2–4 lines per hotspot response.** Three is the sweet spot.
- **True rhymes only.** Say it aloud. *Moon / June* yes. *Moon / room* no. *Glass / grass* yes. *Glass / class* yes. *Glass / pass* no, the consonant cluster differs.
- **Per-hotspot:** at least 3 distinct responses, varying in subject, tone, and length. A 4th rare response (every 7th click) where possible — make it the most surprising of the four (a remix, a cameo, a longer poem).

## What to avoid

| Don't | Why |
|---|---|
| "Click on the bear!" | Instructs instead of inviting. The world invites; we never command. |
| "Great job!" "You did it!" | Praise inflation. Reward with content, not flattery. |
| "Once upon a time..." / "Twinkle twinkle..." | Cliché. We're making something fresh. |
| "Like, totally," "OMG," etc. | Modern slang dates the work. The voice is timeless. |
| Near-rhymes for convenience | Children hear true rhymes; near-rhymes feel sloppy even if they don't know why. |
| "The sun goes to sleep at night" | False oversimplifications create unlearning later. |
| "If you're scared, you can leave!" | We don't acknowledge fear states; nothing is scary. |

## What to do more of

- **Surprise the ear.** A line should land somewhere unexpected (an image, a fact, a question).
- **Smuggle a fact.** "A butterfly tastes with the soles of her feet" is a real thing. Children love real things.
- **Use specific, concrete words.** *daisy* over *flower*, *ochre* over *yellow-brown*, *teaspoon* over *small tool*. Tiny words lose music.
- **Invent words sparingly.** A *snorkleberry* is fine if it earns its keep — Seuss's nonsense words always fit the meter and the joke. Random invention is just noise.
- **End on a turn.** The best couplets pivot in the last line. Setup, setup, twist.

## The Mister-Rogers test

Would a kind grown-up be comfortable reading this aloud to a child? Would they smile or wince? If they'd wince, rewrite. Heavy themes (where people come from, why things end, why some people are sad) handled like a children's book that *respects the reader*: warm, honest, never grim, never falsely cheerful.

## Worked examples

**Good:**
> *The butterfly bobo, with wings made of glass,*
> *Drinks dew from the daisies and dances on grass.*
> *He's small, but don't tell him — he won't quite agree.*
> *He thinks he's the largest of all things to see.*

Anapestic, true rhymes, vivid image, real fact, ends on character.

**Bad (forced rhyme):**
> *The butterfly is so pretty and nice,*
> *Click on him please, that would be twice!*

Instructional, breaks fourth wall, no image, the rhyme is a noun and a numeral.

**Bad (greeting card):**
> *Friends are friends and friends are great,*
> *Friends will always lift your weight!*

Vague, abstract, lecturing. No image. No surprise.

**Good (lesson smuggled):**
> *They asked the old turtle what made the world turn,*
> *And he blinked very slowly, then said, "You will learn."*
> *Some questions take seconds. Some questions take years.*
> *And some? Well, you carry them all your life, dears.*

Philosophy, in voice, no preaching.

## Last check before committing a line

- [ ] Meter is consistent across the stanza.
- [ ] All rhymes are true rhymes.
- [ ] No instructions, no praise, no clichés, no slang.
- [ ] Vocabulary is A1 / Dolch pre-K + K, *or* the unfamiliar word is contextually obvious and earns a place.
- [ ] If there's a lesson, it's true and won't need to be unlearned later.
- [ ] Read aloud once. Did you smile? Did the child you imagine smile?
